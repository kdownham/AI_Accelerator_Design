MAD-X Upright Quadrupole Flat-Beam Transformer Summary

Comparison of the current upright-quadrupole-only design family for a 5 TeV electron beam. Beam sizes assume a geometric RMS emittance of 20 nm in both transverse planes.

The clean baseline is upright_length_alpha_8q: it exactly matches BETX = 80 m, BETY = 30 m, and endpoint alphas near zero with moderate gradients. The most aperture-friendly accepted candidate is upright_peak_reduced_8q, which cuts the maximum RMS beam size by about a factor of two while preserving the endpoint optics, at the cost of pushing one quadrupole to about 170 T/m.

Interpretation

Zero-alpha baseline

upright_length_alpha_8q is the reference design to keep handy. It has a very clean endpoint match and moderate maximum gradient, but the horizontal beta peak is large enough to give a maximum RMS beam size of about 4.55 cm.

Peak-reduced 8Q

upright_peak_reduced_8q is the best endpoint-matched aperture improvement. It reduces the maximum RMS beam size to about 2.38 cm. The main tradeoff is gradient margin: one quadrupole reaches the 170 T/m cap used in that optimization.

Lower-gradient 8Q

upright_peak_reduced_8q_g160 gives nearly the same endpoint match with a more conservative gradient cap near 160 T/m. Its maximum RMS beam size is slightly larger than the peak-reduced 8Q, but it may be the more practical engineering starting point.

10Q trial

upright_peak_reduced_10q has the smallest maximum RMS beam size in this set, but it is not as well matched at the endpoint. The final beta values are close but not exact, and the endpoint alphas are small rather than essentially zero.

Optimization Approaches Used

  1. Upright-only lattice search. The earlier skew/solenoid concepts were set aside for this branch, and the lattice was redesigned using normal upright quadrupoles only. A six-quadrupole compromise was first found, then positions and gradients were allowed to vary.
  2. Position-varied 8Q search. A fast transfer-matrix optimizer was added to explore quadrupole positions and gradients without paying the full MAD-X runtime cost on every trial. The useful candidates were then verified with MAD-X.
  3. Length and gradient least-squares rematch. The endpoint alpha problem was solved by varying the lattice length and quadrupole gradients while keeping the 8Q position pattern fixed. This produced upright_length_alpha_8q, with BETX/BETY = 80/30 m and endpoint alphas numerically zero.
  4. Constrained peak beam-size minimization. Starting from the zero-alpha baseline, a constrained SLSQP optimizer minimized a high-order norm of the sampled RMS beam size while enforcing endpoint BETX, BETY, ALFX, and ALFY. This produced the peak-reduced 8Q designs.
  5. Gradient-cap trade study. The peak-size optimizer was repeated with a tighter gradient cap near 160 T/m. This produced a slightly larger beam envelope but better magnet-strength margin.
  6. Additional-quadrupole trial. Two extra upright quadrupole slots were introduced to test whether added degrees of freedom could reduce peak beam size further. The resulting 10Q trial is promising for aperture, but it was not rematched as cleanly as the 8Q solutions under the tighter gradient cap.

Current Recommendation

Keep upright_length_alpha_8q as the clean matched reference and continue near upright_peak_reduced_8q or upright_peak_reduced_8q_g160 for aperture-driven iteration. The next useful iteration would be to preserve the tight endpoint match while reducing the highest gradients, especially the first two quadrupoles in the peak-reduced designs.
